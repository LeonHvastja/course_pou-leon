Principles of Uncertainty – exercises

Chapter 2 Uncountable probability spaces

This chapter deals with uncountable probability spaces.

The students are expected to acquire the following knowledge:

Theoretical

  • Understand Borel sets and identify them.
  • Estimate Lebesgue measure for different sets.
  • Know when sets are Borel-measurable.
  • Understanding of countable and uncountable sets.

R

  • Uniform sampling.

2.1 Borel sets

Exercise 1.1 a. Prove that the intersection of two sigma algebras on \(\Omega\) is a sigma algebra.

  1. Prove that the collection of all open subsets \((a,b)\) on \((0,1]\) is not a sigma algebra of \((0,1]\).

Exercise 1.2 Show that \(\mathcal{C} = \sigma(\mathcal{C})\) if and only if \(\mathcal{C}\) is a sigma algebra.

Exercise 1.3 Let \(\mathcal{C}\) and \(\mathcal{D}\) be two collections of subsets on \(\Omega\) such that \(\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{D}\). Prove that \(\sigma(\mathcal{C}) \subset \sigma(\mathcal{D})\).

Exercise 1.4 Prove that the following subsets of \((0,1]\) are Borel-measurable.

  1. Any countable set.

  2. The set of numbers in (0,1] whose decimal expansion does not contain 7.

Exercise 1.5 Let \(\Omega = [0,1]\), and let \(\mathcal{F}_3\) consist of all countable subsets of \(\Omega\), and all subsets of \(\Omega\) having a countable complement.

  1. Show that \(\mathcal{F}_3\) is a sigma algebra.

  2. Let us define \(P(A)=0\) if \(A\) is countable, and \(P(A) = 1\) if \(A\) has a countable complement. Is \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}_3, P)\) a legitimate probability space?

2.2 Lebesgue measure

Exercise 1.6 Show that the Lebesgue measure of rational numbers on \([0,1]\) is 0. R: Implement a random number generator, which generates uniform samples of irrational numbers in \([0,1]\) by uniformly sampling from \([0,1]\) and rejecting a sample if it is rational.

Exercise 1.7

  1. Prove that the Lebesgue measure of \(\mathbb{R}\) is infinity.

  2. Paradox. Show that the cardinality of \(\mathbb{R}\) and \((0,1)\) is the same, while their Lebesgue measures are infinity and one respectably.

Exercise 2.1 Take the measure space \((\Omega_1 = (0,1], B_{(0,1], \lambda})\) (we know that this is a probability space on \((0,1]\)).

  1. Define an injective map (function) from \(\Omega_1\) to \(\Omega_2 = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) such that the measure space \((\Omega_2, 2^{\Omega_2}, \lambda(f^{-1}()))\) will be a discrete probability space with uniform probabilities (\(P(\omega) = \frac{1}{6}, \forall \omega \in \Omega_2)\).
  2. Is the map that you defined in (a) the only such map?
  3. How would you in the same fashion define an injective map that would result in a probability space that can be interpreted as a coin toss with probability \(p\) of heads?
  4. R: Use the map in (a) as a basis for a random generator for this fair die.
